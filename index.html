01 — About

Bridging robotics and the recovering brain

I am a postdoctoral researcher at Northwestern University and the Shirley Ryan AbilityLab, working with Dr. William Z. Rymer on spasticity in stroke survivors. My current research develops quantitative devices that make spasticity assessment faster and more precise than conventional clinical tests, and investigates pharmacological treatments with fewer side effects than existing options.

My work sits at the intersection of robotics, neuroscience, and rehabilitation medicine. During my Ph.D. at DGIST, I designed patented upper-limb rehabilitation exoskeletons that deliver multiple therapeutic movements with a single motor, and used fNIRS neuroimaging to ground robot-assisted therapy in measured brain activity. At KAIST, I built immersive VR- and rhythm-game-based hand rehabilitation systems that keep patients engaged throughout therapy.

Trained across Japan (Ritsumeikan, Osaka), South Korea (DGIST, KAIST), and the United States, my long-term goal is to lead an interdisciplinary research program that pushes the boundaries of rehabilitation science — and to mentor the next generation of researchers doing it.

Quantifying & treating spasticity

Devices using controlled tendon indentation and 3D force-vector analysis for faster, more precise spasticity assessment — paired with pilot studies of low-side-effect medications such as cyproheptadine.
